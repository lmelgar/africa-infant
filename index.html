The Saddest Record

About 5.9 million children under the age of five will die this year, according to UNICEF. This means that every minute, 11 children will die, most of them in Africa. Although most developing countries have decreased their infant mortality rate in the last decade, sub-Saharan Africa faces a major challenge: the continent's infant mortality rate is 15 times higher than the average for developed regions. Diarrhea and pneumonia are two of the leading causes of infant mortality.

UNICEF defines this rate as the "probability of dying between birth and exactly 5 years of age, expressed per 1,000 live births."



Who ranks the worst

Angola and Somalia tops the children mortality rate in Africa. But in other countries, like Sierra Leone, Central African Republica, Chad or South Sudan the number of children who die every year is huge.

A declining process

Only in 2013, 1,5 million children died because of diarrhea and pneumonia. 71% of the deaths were in 15 countries, according to the International Vaccine Access Center. Despite its deadly impact, infant mortality due to diarrhea and pneumonia has decreased in the last decade.

Water and treatment

According to UNICEF, factors as the use of improved water or some kind of treatments can help to reduce the infant mortality rate provoked by diarrhoea and pneumonia. Who has access to the water? Who is receiving those treatments?

ORS

The oral rehydration salts (ORS) treatment reduces the effects of diarrhoea. It helps the children to absorb water and protects them from future episodes.

In African countries, a high percentage of people live in rural areas

People in these areas have limited access to improved drinking water. In Angola, for instance, the access in rural zones is only 28%. This country has the largest infant mortality rate worldwide.

In urban areas, the picture is completely different. However, in Mauritania, only 58% of the population has access to improved drinking-water.

But not always urban means progress. Sub-Sahara African urban areas lack access to improved sanitation-water, which means that they are not "protected from outside contamination, particularly faecal matter."

And this gets worse in the rural areas.

In Somalia, the country with the highest rate of children killed by diarrhoea, only 6% of girls and 7% of boys get treatment.

Treatment is more prevalent in urban zones. Djibouti is an exception: 63% of children in rural areas were treated with ORS, versus 32% in the urban ones.

In Chad, Nigeria and Burkina Faso, the gap between children from rich families receiving ORS and the poor ones are the highest in Africa.

In regards to the treatment of penumonia, Somalia also ranks as the worst. On the other hand, in Uganda, 83% of girls and 75% of boys are treated for this disease.

In most countries, the percentage of children treated for pneumonia is almost the same, no matter whether it's in rural or urban areas. But not in Malawi or Ghana, where more children are treated in rural zones. In Guinea or Chad, it's the other way around.

In most sub-Saharan countries, being rich means having access to pneumonia treatment. But in Gambia, Ghana or Senegal, more poor children than rich ones were treated.
