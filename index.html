The Saddest Record

An estimated of 5.9 million children under five-year-old will die in 2015, according to UNICEF. This means that every minute, 11 children will die. Most of them, in Africa. Although most of the developing countries have decreased their infant mortality rate within the last decade, Sub-Saharan Africa faces a big challenge: the continent's rate is 15 times the average for developed regions. Among the causes, two of them share the saddest record: diarrhoea and pneumonia, the most deadliest illness.

Hover over the countries to see rate of children under five years old dead in 2015. Click on them to see how their rate has changed during de last 15 years. UNICEF defines this rate as the "probability of dying between birth and exactly 5 years of age, expressed per 1,000 live births."



Who ranks the worst

Angola and Somalia tops the children mortality rate in Africa. But in other countries, like Sierra Leone, Central African Republica, Chad or South Sudan the number of children who die every year is huge.

A declining process

Only in 2013, 1.5 million children died because of diarrhoea and pneumonia. 71% of them just in 15 countries, according to International Vaccine Access Center. Although the mortal presence of diarrhoea and the acute respiratory infections -which pneumonia-, during the last decade, both has decreased.

This graphic shows the top 15 infant mortality rate provoked by these illnesses, and its evolution since 2000.

The factors

According to UNICEF, factors as the use of improved water or some kind of treatments can help to reduce the infant mortality rate provoked by diarrhoea and pneumonia. Who has access to the water? Who is receiving those treatments?

In African countries a high percentage of people live in rural areas

People in this areas have a limited access to improved drinking water. In Angola, the access in rural zones is only 28%. The country has the largest infant mortality rate worldwide.

In the urban areas, the picture is completely different. However, in Mauritania, only the 58% of the population has access to improved drinking-water.

But not always urban means progress. African urban areas lack of access to improved sanitation-water, which means that "are protected from outside contamination, particularly faecal matter."

And this gets worse in the rural areas.
